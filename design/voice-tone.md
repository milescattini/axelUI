# Voice & Tone Guide — Axel

## Who this is for

SMB founders and directors who are setting up (or cleaning up) their company structure. They're not lawyers and don't want to become one. They're capable, busy, and slightly anxious about getting something wrong — company constitution, shareholder agreement, director duties, ASIC lodgements — the stuff that feels opaque until you've done it once.

They don't need hand-holding. They need the real version, fast.

---

## The voice in one sentence

> The friend who set up their third company last year and will tell you what actually matters, not what a lawyer would charge you to explain.

---

## Personality axes

```
Formal    ←————————●————— Casual
Technical ←——————●——————— Plain English
Authority ←————————————●— Peer-to-peer
```

**Casual** — not sloppy. Contractions are fine. Sentences can be short. You don't need to sound like a government form.

**Plain English** — name the real thing (shareholder agreement, vesting cliff, replaceable rules) but don't hide behind it. If a term needs unpacking, unpack it in the same breath.

**Peer-to-peer** — no "we're here to help you on your journey." You're talking to someone who runs a business, not someone who needs to be guided through a wizard.

---

## Tone by context

The voice stays constant. The tone shifts with what's at stake.

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Hero / headline | Blunt, confident | "Most founders skip the shareholders agreement. That's the expensive mistake." |
| Explanatory body | Informative, direct | "A vesting cliff means your co-founder doesn't own their shares until they've been around for 12 months. Standard is 1 year cliff, 4 year total." |
| Edge cases / warnings | Honest, not alarmist | "If you're using replaceable rules, you're relying on the Corporations Act defaults. That's fine for a solo operator. Two or more founders and you want something written down." |
| CTA | Specific, low pressure | "See what a constitution actually covers" / "Get the checklist" |
| Error / dead end | Matter-of-fact | "That doesn't exist yet — here's what's close." |

## The four writing rules

### 1. Name the real thing
Use actual terms — ABN, ASIC, Pty Ltd, shareholders agreement, vesting, constitution, replaceable rules. Don't euphemise into "your business structure" or "legal documentation."

Readers google these terms. Using them correctly builds trust and helps SEO.

### 2. Lead with the consequence, not the feature
The reader doesn't care what the product does. They care what happens if they get it wrong — or right.

- ✗ "Our governance checklist covers all the key areas of company setup."
- ✓ "Most disputes between co-founders come back to one thing: they never wrote down who owns what if someone leaves."

### 3. Be specific or don't say it
Vague claims land nowhere. Every sentence should contain at least one of: a tool name, a number, a step, a time, a consequence, or a named scenario.

- ✗ "We make the process simple and straightforward."
- ✓ "Company registration through ASIC takes about 15 minutes. The shareholders agreement takes longer — here's a starting point."

### 4. Acknowledge the hard parts
This audience is smart. Pretending governance is simple reads as naive or dishonest. Acknowledge complexity briefly, then move through it.

- ✗ "Getting your company structure right is easy with Axel."
- ✓ "The structure question — Pty Ltd vs trust vs sole trader — depends on your situation. Here's how to think through it."

## Banned words and phrases

These trigger immediate distrust with an SMB audience. Ban them absolutely.

| Banned | Why | Use instead |
|---|---|---|
| empower | Vague, patronising | Say what you actually give them |
| seamless | Always a lie | Describe what's actually easy |
| unlock | Startup cliché | Just say what becomes possible |
| navigate | Implies they're lost | They're not — they just need information |
| journey | Condescending | Omit it |
| leverage | Jargon | Use, apply, get more from |
| robust | Meaningless filler | Specific claim or nothing |
| solution(s) | Hollow noun | Name the actual thing |
| streamline | Lazy | Describe the actual improvement |
| game-changer | Hyperbole | Cut it |
| holistic | Usually means incomplete | Omit or be specific |
| ecosystem | Startup jargon | Omit |

## Before / after examples

**Hero headline**

- ✗ "Empowering founders to navigate the complexities of business governance"
- ✓ "Getting your company structure right takes a day. Most people guess and fix it later."

**Feature description**

- ✗ "Our shareholders agreement template covers all the key scenarios your business might face."
- ✓ "The template covers vesting, IP assignment, decision-making thresholds, and what happens if a founder leaves. Those are the four things that cause problems."

**Edge case warning**

- ✗ "Please note that individual circumstances may vary and you should seek professional advice."
- ✓ "If you have more than two shareholders or any external investment, get a lawyer to review this. The template is a starting point, not a substitute."

**CTA**

- ✗ "Get started today and unlock your business potential"
- ✓ "Download the checklist — 12 things to sort in your first 90 days"

## Australian English and context

Always:
- AUD, GST, BAS, ABN, ACN, Pty Ltd, ASIC, the Corporations Act
- "licence" not "license" (noun form)
- "organisation" not "organization"
- Refer to tax as tax, not "taxes" — the Australian framing is singular

Never fabricate:
- No made-up client stories or testimonials
- No invented metrics ("saves founders 40 hours")
- Illustrative framing is fine: "A founder who skips the shareholders agreement and then falls out with their co-founder…"

---

## What this voice is not

- Not a legal service. Don't imply otherwise. Be clear about what a template is versus advice.
- Not a startup hype machine. No references to "building something great" or "your vision."
- Not a government brochure. No passive voice, no hedging every sentence.
- Not a chatbot. Write like a person.
